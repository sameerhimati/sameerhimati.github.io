I've been using Claude Code almost every day for the past year. In the last 40 days I've shipped more than I did in the previous 6 months. An iOS app, multiple web apps, an entire knowledge management system, skills, automations. All from a terminal.

But here's the thing I keep coming back to.

I don't fully understand how it works.

I know how to USE agents. I'm good at it. I can write prompts, build skills, chain tools together, get things done. But if you asked me to BUILD one from scratch...how do you spawn an agent with a specific role? How do you manage its context? How do you orchestrate multiple agents that coordinate on a task? How was Claude Code actually built? Could I build something like it with an open source model?

I don't have good answers to those questions yet. And I think I need to.

---

Here's what I see happening.

The way humans interact with software is being rewritten. Right now. Not in some theoretical future — right now. I wrote about this a month ago, how the CLI is cool again, how we're coming full circle back to text-based interfaces. But it goes deeper than that.

Every AI company right now is selling you a chatbot. A text box. You type, it responds. That's it. But the technology for AI that actually DOES things — that sends your emails, manages your calendar, monitors your investments, operates software on your behalf — that technology exists today. The agents work. I've seen them work. I use them every day.

The gap isn't the technology. It's that almost nobody outside of developers has access to it. My girlfriend spends 5-10 hours a week triaging emails and coordinating tasks across disconnected tools. My dad runs a dental clinic and checks dashboards manually for information an agent could surface in a text message. These are smart people. They just don't live in a terminal.

I want to close that gap. But first I need to understand the machinery well enough to rebuild it.

---

So here's what I'm going to do.

I'm going to learn how AI agents actually work. From the ground up. The full stack — how models reason, how tool use works, how agents are spawned and controlled, how systems like Claude Code and OpenClaw are architected, whether open source models can match proprietary ones for agentic tasks, and what it takes to make all of this accessible to people who aren't developers.

And I'm going to write about everything I find.

Not tutorials. Not "10 best AI tools" listicles. Just what I'm learning, what I'm building, what works, what doesn't, and what I think it means.

I'm doing this for three reasons.

**One, writing forces clarity.** I can't bullshit my way through a blog post the way I can through a conversation. If I don't understand something, the writing exposes it.

**Two, I think this matters.** Agents are going to change how every person interacts with software. Not in 5 years. Now. And most people writing about it are either selling something or summarizing press releases. I want to write from the inside — from actually building the things.

**Three, I'm building something.** I don't have the full picture yet. But I know the direction. Every project I've worked on for the past year — real estate AI, clinic management, browser agents, background orchestration systems — has been circling the same idea: one general-purpose agent engine that can be pointed at any domain with the right connectors. I'm getting closer to what that product looks like. Writing is how I'll figure out the rest.

---

If you're curious about the same questions — how agents work, what you can build with them, and where this is all going — follow along. I'll be posting here and probably talking about it way too much on X.

Let's see where this goes.
